# Blurby TTS Architecture Completion Audit

## Executive verdict

The roadmap is **directionally promising but not dispatch-safe as currently written**. The strongest part of the plan is the middle of the conveyor: integrating the unmerged sync and diagnostics work, then hardening cache and timing contracts before building more sync logic. That sequence is well supported by the code and by the implementation review. The weakest parts are governance fidelity, current-state truthfulness, and missing foundational modeling. The primary roadmap artifact supplied for audit, `ROADMAP.md`, does not actually contain all eight readable full sprint specifications because it ends mid-sentence during `TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1`; later sprint details had to be reconstructed from `SPRINT_QUEUE.md` and the PM2 planning artifacts. That alone means the roadmap, as an audit object, is incomplete. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 522–644; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 61–76 and 206–329; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/2026-05-15-pm2-plan.md`, lines 63–80; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/2026-05-15-pm2-phase-brief.md`, lines 7–16 and 50–71.)

My bottom-line answer to the primary question, “Given the current codebase and documentation, is this the right plan, in the right order, for the right reasons?”, is **not yet**. It is the **mostly right plan**, but **not in a fully correct or sufficiently explicit order**, and **not documented with enough internal consistency to treat as the source of execution truth**. The roadmap correctly prioritizes `TTS-INTEGRATE-1` and `TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1`, and it correctly identifies event-driven sync, normalizer enrichment, and render-time indexing as the major remaining architecture themes. But it under-models two prerequisites that its own supporting research treats as important: a durable segment identity model and an alignment-aware normalization contract. It also delays cross-module contract testing too long, and several planning documents contradict each other about what `TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1` actually includes. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_Kokoro_TTS_Implementation_Review_2026-05-15.md`, lines 20–21 and 335–346; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_TTS_Literature_Codebase_Review_2026-05-11.md`, lines 578–587 and 644–656; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/2026-05-15-pm2-phase-brief.md`, lines 52–71; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 629–640.)

The roadmap also overstates the present state of the system. In current code, Blurby is **not yet Kokoro-only**. `MOSS-Nano` and `Pocket TTS` are still selectable in the provider registry, still routed by `useNarration`, and still exposed through live `tts-nano-*` and `tts-pocket-*` IPC handlers. Only Qwen currently follows the fully disabled pattern. The Technical Reference still describes Nano as “recommended opt-in” and Pocket TTS as “available opt-in,” which is consistent with the code and inconsistent with the orientation material’s Kokoro-only framing. That matters because `ENGINE-DORMANCY-1` is not a cleanup sprint over already-dormant code; it is a real product-posture change that has not landed yet. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/ttsProviderRegistry.ts`, lines 94–151; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/useNarration.ts`, lines 1064–1091, 1259–1288, and 1504–1607; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/ipc_tts.js`, lines 177–287; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/TECHNICAL_REFERENCE.md`, lines 416–426.)

## Current-state architecture summary

The current TTS architecture is already materially advanced. Kokoro inference runs in a dedicated worker thread; the renderer uses `createGenerationPipeline` to plan and produce chunks; `createAudioScheduler` pre-schedules Web Audio playback and computes per-word boundaries from real timestamps when they validate, or falls back to heuristic timing when they do not; and the cache layer already supports schema-versioned v2 identities with timing sidecars and atomic writes. This is not an immature subsystem waiting for first principles. It is a fairly sophisticated Kokoro-centered stack that now needs contract consolidation more than conceptual reinvention. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b4/tts-worker.js`, lines 45–121 and 151–165; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/generationPipeline.ts`, lines 399–526 and 540–614; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/audioScheduler.ts`, lines 351–423, 536–605, and 765–801; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/tts-cache.js`, lines 98–166, 193–222, and 298–365.)

**What already exists.** The codebase already has a provider metadata registry, a spoken-text normalizer, structured cache identity, timing sidecars, chunk planning with pause-aware boundaries, Kokoro rate bucketing, scheduler truth callbacks, and a split main/renderer architecture. `kokoroStrategy.ts` normalizes spoken text before generation and constructs a v2 cache identity with provider, voice, rate bucket, model version, source and normalized hashes, normalizer version, override hash, `chunkId`, sample rate, and `timingTruth`; `generationPipeline.ts` then emits scheduled chunks and writes timing metadata into the cache; `audioScheduler.ts` validates native timestamps and falls back honestly when needed. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b4/kokoroStrategy.ts`, lines 144–185 and 239–255; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/segmentNormalizer.ts`, lines 339–386; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsCache.ts`, lines 10–24 and 28–63; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/generationPipeline.ts`, lines 460–526; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/audioScheduler.ts`, lines 358–423.)

**What the roadmap assumes exists, but does not yet exist on current `main`.** The planning documents explicitly state that `TTS-SYNC-1` and `TTS-DIAG-1` are complete on branches but not merged to canonical `main`. The current batch confirms that absence indirectly: the provided `narrateDiagnostics.ts` is still a lightweight snapshot/event utility, not the redacted `tts-diagnostics-v1` bundle described in the branch closeout, and the batch does not include `timingMetadataStore.ts` or `highlightSyncController.ts`, which the governance documents say are branch-complete. Later sprints that rely on those abstractions are therefore rightly blocked by `TTS-INTEGRATE-1`. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/CLAUDE.md`, lines 345–349; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 354–356; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/narrateDiagnostics.ts`, lines 7–90.)

**What is actually missing.** The most important missing architectural element is not just “event-driven sync.” It is a durable, first-class representation of segment identity and alignment. The literature review flags deterministic segment IDs as a **high-severity, P0 gap**, and recommends a `NarrationSegment` abstraction; the current code does not have one. The existing `chunkId` is a cache-derived identifier built as `${bookId}:${startIdx}:${normalization.normalizationHash}`. Because `normalizationHash` includes normalizer version, source hash, normalized hash, override hash, and transform list, the same logical reading segment can get a different `chunkId` whenever normalization rules evolve. That is useful for cache invalidation, but unsuitable as a durable export, subtitle, or highlight anchor. `TtsCacheDocumentLocator` even allows `sectionId` and `cfi`, but `kokoroStrategy.ts` currently populates only `{ bookId }`. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_TTS_Literature_Codebase_Review_2026-05-11.md`, lines 578–587; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsCache.ts`, lines 4–24; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/kokoroStrategy.ts`, lines 151–168; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/segmentNormalizer.ts`, lines 365–386.)

**What has to happen before later items can succeed.** Three things are true from the evidence. First, `TTS-INTEGRATE-1` must land before any downstream sprint that expects `TimingMetadataStore`, `HighlightSyncController`, or the redacted diagnostics bundle to be on `main`. Second, `TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1` must happen before event-driven sync because cache hits currently rehydrate materially less metadata than fresh generation. Third, some form of alignment-and-segment foundation must exist before the combination of `TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1`, `NORMALIZER-ENRICH-1`, and “export-ready” can be considered technically coherent. The current roadmap captures the first two dependencies well, but not the third. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 625–633; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/utils_ttsCache.ts`, lines 42–55; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/generationPipeline.ts`, lines 496–506 and 513–523; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_Kokoro_TTS_Implementation_Review_2026-05-15.md`, lines 20–21 and 357–369.)

## Roadmap item-by-item audit

**ENGINE-DORMANCY-1.** This sprint is grounded in real code. Nano and Pocket are live today at the registry, runtime, and orchestration levels, and Qwen provides a concrete disabled pattern to emulate. The sprint is therefore directionally correct. However, the spec is slightly imprecise against the actual type model. The registry does not have a typed `posture` enum; it has a descriptive `copy.posture` string plus operational fields such as `selectable`, `disabledReason`, and `statusKind`. Saying “set posture to dormant” is not wrong conceptually, but it is sloppy relative to the current code. More importantly, the sprint should be framed as a **product-scope reduction** and **test-lane unblocker**, not as if the code were already dormant. That distinction matters for change management and for evaluating regressions. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsProvider.ts`, lines 16–52; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/ttsProviderRegistry.ts`, lines 65–151; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/ipc_tts.js`, lines 177–287; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 551–585; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 122–147.)

**TTS-INTEGRATE-1.** This is the most obviously necessary sprint after whatever unblocker is chosen. The governance material is consistent that `TTS-SYNC-1` and `TTS-DIAG-1` are done on branches but not on `main`, and the current source package aligns with that. The sprint is logically coherent, well bounded, and sequenced correctly ahead of downstream architecture work. My only reservation is that the plan ties unblocking entirely to engine dormancy, when the deeper technical blocker is really “default `npm test` cannot clear the Nano probe lane.” Dormancy is sufficient, but not the only conceivable unblocker. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/CLAUDE.md`, lines 345–349; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 150–172 and 354–356; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 587–618.)

**TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1.** This is the strongest sprint in the conveyor. It is precisely grounded in the code. Renderer-side cache rehydration returns only `audio`, `sampleRate`, `durationMs`, `words`, `startIdx`, and `wordTimestamps`, while fresh chunks include fields like `weightConfig`, `boundaryType`, and `silenceMs`. `ScheduledChunk` does not currently carry `timingTruth` or `chunkId`. Timing truth is also represented in overlapping places: the canonical enum in `ttsProvider.ts`, the sidecar’s `timingTruth` and `timingClassification`, and manifest metadata in `main/tts-cache.js`. On top of that, `isStructuredIdentity` only validates `{ schemaVersion: 2 }`, `ipc_tts.js` passes raw cache payloads through without shape checks, `runPipeline()` is launched without a `.catch()`, `pipelineResume()` flushes the pause buffer without backpressure checks, and telemetry widens through `as any`. This sprint is not optional; it is a necessary contract-hardening gate. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/utils_ttsCache.ts`, lines 26–55; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/audioScheduler.ts`, lines 16–24, 79–103, 375–383, and 412–419; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsProvider.ts`, lines 4–8; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsCache.ts`, lines 36–63; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/tts-cache.js`, lines 67–69, 193–222, 341–355, and 377–400; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/ipc_tts.js`, lines 335–348; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/generationPipeline.ts`, lines 618–623 and 654–663.)

**TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1.** The goal is right, but the spec is the least stable conceptually. The current system already has scheduler-originated `onWordAdvance`, `onTruthSync`, and `getAudioProgress()`. Those callbacks are derived by the scheduler’s own `AudioContext.currentTime` loop, which is intentionally authoritative. The lessons learned explicitly codify that the RAF glide loop must read only from scheduler-owned `getAudioProgress()`, and that `lastConfirmedAudioWordRef` must be written only by scheduler-confirmed boundary events. The sprint spec’s language about “replacing RAF polling with word-boundary events” is therefore easy to misread. If it means “remove render-layer polling for discrete word decisions while retaining scheduler-authoritative interpolation,” it is coherent. If it means “remove RAF as a core visual transport,” it risks violating the system’s own guardrails. The chosen phrase should be narrowed. The second problem is A9: the code has a `TransformFn = (text: string) => string` normalizer, and the implementation review explicitly says extensibility does **not** imply alignment readiness. The spec’s Phase 0 gate is a good mitigation, but it confirms the underlying modeling gap rather than solving it. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/audioScheduler.ts`, lines 429–489 and 750–801; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/useNarration.ts`, lines 141–151, 418–423, 975–1000, and 1739–1745; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/LESSONS_LEARNED.md`, lines 855–870 and 893–907; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/segmentNormalizer.ts`, lines 48 and 339–386; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_Kokoro_TTS_Implementation_Review_2026-05-15.md`, lines 20–21 and 338–339; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 206–237.)

**NORMALIZER-ENRICH-1.** This sprint addresses a real and well-documented gap. The current normalizer implements 12 transforms, all English-first, and it clearly lacks the broader coverage described by the research and implementation review, especially around decimals, fractions, URLs, address abbreviations, ranges, and heteronyms. The code is structurally easy to extend in the narrow sense that adding transforms is simple. The problem is that enrichment after event-sync makes the alignment contract more important, not less. So the sprint is technically correct, but it should not be allowed to proceed unless the alignment model is either first-class or already proven with adversarial fixtures. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/segmentNormalizer.ts`, lines 5–17, 158–169, and 339–386; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_Kokoro_TTS_Implementation_Review_2026-05-15.md`, lines 16 and 338–339; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 240–260.)

**TTS-RENDER-MAP-1.** This sprint is plausible and probably valuable, but it is the least directly verifiable from the supplied code subset because the actual Foliate render component is not included in the batch files I could inspect. The research provenance is sound, and the concept is coherent: pre-build word-to-DOM position lookup and stop paying repeated DOM query costs during narration. What is under-specified is invalidation. A word-position index must clearly define rebuild triggers for pagination changes, font changes, viewport changes, section changes, annotation overlays, and any render-layer re-tokenization. The sprint spec mentions integration and invalidation wiring, but not enough to make performance claims such as “5–8ms down to ≤2ms p95” unambiguous from the materials supplied. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 264–285; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_TTS_Literature_Codebase_Review_2026-05-11.md`, lines 170–180 and 236–249.)

**TTS-PIPELINE-1.** The sprint is necessary, but it is under-sized and too late. As described, it wants to trace planner → normalizer → cache identity → timing sidecar → word-boundary event → word position index, verify cache-hit parity, add stress fixtures, and expand normalization fixtures. That is not a “small” sprint in any meaningful program-audit sense. Some of that testing, especially the contract tests around alignment and cache-hit parity, belongs earlier as gates for the sprints that introduce those contracts. The PM2 plan also says this sprint includes a unified `NarrationSegment` domain-type assessment, but that obligation is not visible in the accessible queue summary for the sprint, which is another sign of planning drift. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 289–308; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/2026-05-15-pm2-plan.md`, lines 68–72; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/2026-05-15-pm2-phase-brief.md`, lines 15–16 and 67–71.)

**TTS-ARCH-DOC-1.** This is reasonable as a final consolidation sprint, but it cannot be the first moment when architectural truth gets repaired. The planning and governance materials are already diverging from the code. A final document is useful, but some ADR-style truth maintenance is needed earlier, especially because the current `ROADMAP.md` is incomplete, the orientation framing overstates current dormancy, and the cache-hardening scope differs across planning artifacts. The sprint itself is fine; treating it as the only governance correction point is not. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 312–331; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/2026-05-15-pm2-phase-brief.md`, lines 15–16 and 69–71; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 522–644.)

## Major contradictions and sequencing analysis

The first major contradiction is the **roadmap’s own auditability**. `SPRINT_QUEUE.md` says the queue is green, with “8 full specs, 0 stubs,” and points to `ROADMAP.md` as the full master. But the supplied `ROADMAP.md` stops during the third sprint. That means the primary control document for execution is incomplete in the audit package, while the secondary documents claim it is complete. From a technical-program-audit standpoint, this is not a cosmetic issue. It means the roadmap fails its own “single source of truth” test before any engineering work is judged. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 61–76; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 621–644.)

The second contradiction is the **current-state narrative**. The current code and Technical Reference still describe a multi-engine runtime with Kokoro default, Nano recommended opt-in, Pocket opt-in, and Qwen disabled. Yet the orientation framing and some queue language effectively talk as if Kokoro were already the sole active engine and the sidecar engines were already dormant. The code is clear: Nano and Pocket are still selectable, still routed, and still registered in IPC. So the roadmap is being justified partly with future-state language that has not landed. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/ttsProviderRegistry.ts`, lines 94–151; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/useNarration.ts`, lines 1064–1091 and 1259–1288; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/ipc_tts.js`, lines 177–255; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/TECHNICAL_REFERENCE.md`, lines 416–426.)

The third contradiction is inside `TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1` planning itself. The PM2 phase brief maps “triple-storage reduction” into `TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1` and treats it as a criterion-level inclusion, but the readable roadmap criteria for the sprint do not capture that. The code does in fact store timing truth in multiple places: in the identity object, in the timing sidecar, and in manifest metadata. This is not a speculative issue. It is live in the code. That means either the phase brief is overstating what the sprint will do, or the readable roadmap is understating it. Either way, the planning artifacts are not internally coherent. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b2/2026-05-15-pm2-phase-brief.md`, lines 52–62; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 629–640; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/tts-cache.js`, lines 193–222 and 341–355.)

The fourth contradiction is between the roadmap’s “export-ready” finish line and the lack of a **durable segment-identity sprint**. The literature review says deterministic segment IDs are a high-severity gap and recommends adding `NarrationSegment`. The queue’s deferred-lane section says `KOKORO-EXPORT-1` depends on durable segment identity, cache, timing, and highlight truth. But the active conveyor does not contain an explicit sprint that makes segment identity first-class. The nearest thing in current code is a cache-derived `chunkId`, and that identifier is normalization-sensitive, not structure-stable. That is a direct mismatch between stated downstream dependency and planned upstream work. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_TTS_Literature_Codebase_Review_2026-05-11.md`, lines 578–587; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 338–340; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/kokoroStrategy.ts`, lines 151–168; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/segmentNormalizer.ts`, lines 365–386.)

On sequencing, the roadmap is strongest where it says `TTS-CACHE-HARDEN-1` must precede `TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1`. That is correct. Cache hits are currently not equivalent to fresh generation, and later sync logic should not depend on incomplete metadata. It is also correct to put `TTS-INTEGRATE-1` before any work that presumes branch-only abstractions are on `main`. Where the sequencing weakens is after that point. The plan should insert a small but explicit alignment-and-segment foundation step before `TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1`, or make that foundation the mandatory first half of `TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1`. It should also move at least some cross-module contract testing earlier, instead of waiting until `TTS-PIPELINE-1`. As written, the roadmap places major contract mutations in the middle and comprehensive end-to-end verification near the end. That is feasible, but not the safest order. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/utils_ttsCache.ts`, lines 26–55; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/generationPipeline.ts`, lines 496–523; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_Kokoro_TTS_Implementation_Review_2026-05-15.md`, lines 357–369; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 206–237 and 289–308.)

## Structural, testing, and risk review

From a structural and modeling standpoint, the current code has four major truths. First, the scheduler and pipeline are the best parts of the system and should remain the backbone. Second, the provider registry is real but still informational; `useNarration.ts` continues to hard-code engine branches for start, speak, pause, resume, and rate changes, so the registry has not become runtime dispatch. Third, the normalizer is clean and useful, but it is one-way, not alignment-aware. Fourth, the cache identity is strong for invalidation and reuse, but not sufficient as a durable reading/export model. Those truths mean the next phase should be about **contract completion**, not broad architecture churn. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/audioScheduler.ts`, lines 153–183 and 536–605; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/generationPipeline.ts`, lines 28–102 and 399–526; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsProvider.ts`, lines 44–52; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/useNarration.ts`, lines 1064–1091, 1455–1498, and 1504–1607; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/segmentNormalizer.ts`, lines 48 and 339–386.)

The most important modeling gap is that the system still conflates **cache identity**, **playback chunk identity**, and **logical narration segment identity**. The research documents separate these ideas more cleanly than the current code. A cache key should vary whenever normalization, overrides, voice, or rate bucket change. A logical segment ID should not. Right now the code has the former, not the latter. This is why the roadmap’s finish line is not fully coherent with the export dependency it names. It also explains why `TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1` and `TTS-RENDER-MAP-1` are more fragile than they seem: both would be more robust if they talked to a stable segment model instead of a normalization-sensitive cache-derived `chunkId`. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b4/kokoroStrategy.ts`, lines 151–168; `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsCache.ts`, lines 10–24; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_TTS_Literature_Codebase_Review_2026-05-11.md`, lines 508–517 and 578–587.)

The testing posture is adequate in intent but weak in timing and package completeness. The sprint specs consistently call for `npm test`, `npm run typecheck`, and `npm run build`, which is good governance. But the supplied audit package does not include the actual test tree, so the documented test-pass counts could not be independently rerun here. More importantly, the proposed testing strategy is too back-loaded. The event-sync and alignment changes deserve an adversarial contract suite before they are embedded into later normalizer enrichment and render-index work. The queue already hints at this with the Phase 0 fixture proof in `TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1`; that instinct should be strengthened and made into a hard gate, not just a preliminary note. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 216–237 and 289–308; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_Kokoro_TTS_Implementation_Review_2026-05-15.md`, lines 357–369.)

The top risks are concentrated rather than diffuse. The first is **artifact drift**, because the roadmap, queue, phase brief, and current-state docs do not fully agree. The second is **alignment-model failure**, because the roadmap wants to build normalized-to-original mapping on top of a `string -> string` normalizer without first-class per-transform provenance. The third is **modeling debt around segment identity**, which threatens the stated “export-ready” finish line. The fourth is **late discovery risk**, because the broadest integration testing is scheduled after several contract-changing sprints. The fifth is **scope collapse by wording**, especially in `TTS-EVENT-SYNC-1`, where “replace RAF polling” could accidentally destabilize the very scheduler-authoritative separation that LL-077 and LL-079 were written to preserve. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/LESSONS_LEARNED.md`, lines 861–870, 903–907, and 1494–1510; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_Kokoro_TTS_Implementation_Review_2026-05-15.md`, lines 20–21 and 338–343; `/mnt/data/audit/b2/Blurby_TTS_Literature_Codebase_Review_2026-05-11.md`, lines 578–587 and 644–656.)

The missing work is therefore concrete, not generic. The roadmap needs an explicit **segment-and-alignment foundation**, a governance repair pass so `ROADMAP.md` is genuinely executable, earlier cross-module contract tests, and a clarified distinction between scheduler-authoritative discrete events and render-layer interpolation. It would also benefit from enriching `documentLocator` beyond `{ bookId }`, because the type already anticipates `sectionId` and `cfi` but the current Kokoro identity does not use them. Finally, if dormancy is the chosen posture, the runtime should not merely hide Nano and Pocket in settings; it should make their dormancy explicit in the orchestration path and, ideally, reduce unnecessary live branching where safe. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b3/types_ttsCache.ts`, lines 4–24; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/kokoroStrategy.ts`, lines 151–168; `/mnt/data/audit/b4/useNarration.ts`, lines 1064–1091 and 1259–1288; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 122–147 and 338–340.)

## Proposed revised roadmap and final recommendation

A revised roadmap should preserve the good core of the existing conveyor, but it should explicitly repair the missing foundation and the governance drift.

1. **Repair roadmap truth first.** Restore `ROADMAP.md` so it actually contains all sprint bodies, and align it with `SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, `CLAUDE.md`, and the PM2 planning files. Until that is done, the roadmap is not a reliable execution artifact. (Repository files: `/mnt/data/audit/b1/ROADMAP.md`, lines 522–644; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/SPRINT_QUEUE.md`, lines 61–76; `/mnt/data/audit/b1/CLAUDE.md`, lines 338–349.)
